Hong Kong scoring is built around *faan* (番), or pattern points. Each special pattern your winning hand contains is worth a fixed number of faan, and the totals are added together.

A normal table sets a *minimum faan* (often 3) below which a hand can't win, and a *maximum* (often 13, called *full faan*) above which all hands are paid the same.

## A few common patterns

These are the patterns you'll see most often in beginner-level games.

### Common hand (平糊) — 1 faan

A hand made entirely of chows, with a pair that isn't dragons or your seat wind. The most basic shape.

### All pungs (對對糊) — 3 faan

Four pungs (or kongs) and a pair. No chows.

### Mixed one-suit (混一色) — 3 faan

All your tiles are from a single suit *plus* honors. The honors are the "mix".

### Pure one-suit (清一色) — 7 faan

All your tiles are from a single suit, period. No honors.

### Dragon pung — 1 faan each

A pung (or kong) of any dragon, scored on top of whatever else you've made.

### Seat / round wind pung — 1 faan each

A pung of your *seat wind* or the *round wind* scores extra. (More on round wind in a moment.)

## Round and seat winds

A full game runs four "rounds" — east, south, west, north — and during each round, that wind is the *round wind*. Players also have their own *seat wind* (east, south, west, north depending on where they sit). Pungs of these matter more than pungs of "off" winds.

## Paying out

When somebody wins:

- If they won by drawing the winning tile themselves (*self-draw*), all three other players pay them.
- If they won by claiming somebody's discard, the discarder pays the full amount and the other two players pay nothing — or in some house rules, all three pay.

The exact amount paid is set by the table. The faan total is converted to "base units" by an exponential schedule: 1 faan = 1 unit, 3 faan = 2 units, 7 faan = 8 units, full faan (13) = a fixed maximum.

<Callout title="The honest truth">
HK scoring has many rules and house variations. Don't try to internalize all of them at once. Learn the common patterns by name, play a few hands, and look up the rest as it comes up. A real game also has a scorer or an app to handle the arithmetic — you don't need to do it in your head.
</Callout>
