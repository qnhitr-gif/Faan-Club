Once the deal is finished, play begins with East — the dealer — and proceeds counter-clockwise around the table.

## A turn, in three beats

1. **Draw.** Take one tile from the front of the wall. Your hand is now 14 tiles.
2. **Decide.** Look at the new tile. Does it improve your hand? Could it complete a set? Could it be the pair you needed?
3. **Discard.** Place one tile face-up in the center of the table. Your hand is back to 13. Your turn is over.

That's it. Every turn is one tile in, one tile out, until somebody wins.

## What the discard does

The discarded tile sits face-up, visible to everyone. It does two things:

- It's information — every player can see what's been thrown away.
- It's *available* — for one brief moment, before the next player draws, anyone at the table can claim it under specific rules.

This claiming is what *calls* are. Pung, chow, and kong can all be made by claiming a discarded tile from the right player, and they're powerful enough that the next chapter is dedicated to them.

<Callout title="Why this loop is the whole game">
Most of mahjong's depth lives in the *decide* step. A new tile arrives, and you have to choose: keep what you have and throw the new one away? Break up an old plan to commit to a new one? Hold something dangerous because it's the only chance at a high-scoring hand?
</Callout>
