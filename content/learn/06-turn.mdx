Before the first tile is drawn, the table goes through a setup: seats are assigned by dice roll, the wall is built, and East deals 13 tiles to each player with 14 for themselves. From there, the game runs on a simple loop.

## Calls — intercepting a discard

The discarded tile sits face-up briefly before the next draw. In that window, any other player can *call* it — claiming the tile to complete one of their sets. This is what makes mahjong social: a single discard can change four people's plans at once.

There are three calls.

<Compare>
  <CompareSide title="Pung — three of a kind" tone="positive">
    You hold two of a tile. Someone discards the third. Call *pung*, take it, expose all three face-up, and discard one of your other tiles. **Anyone at the table can pung** — it doesn't matter whose turn it was.

### Kong — the fourth tile

If you have three of a tile and someone discards the fourth, call *kong*. Expose all four, then draw a replacement tile from the back of the wall before discarding — the wall effectively compensates you for the extra tile locked up in the kong.

You can also kong on your own turn — if you draw the fourth tile of a triplet already in your hand, declare a *concealed kong*. It scores more generously than an open kong.

## The call trade-off

<Compare>
  <CompareSide title="Call — faster, but visible" tone="cautious">
    Calling skips turn order and locks in a set immediately. The risk: your exposed tiles are visible to everyone. Opponents will stop discarding tiles that help you.
  </CompareSide>
  <CompareSide title="Stay concealed — slower, but hidden" tone="positive">
    A fully concealed hand scores higher than the same hand with exposed sets. Sometimes the right move is to let a useful discard pass and wait for the wall to deliver it naturally.
  </CompareSide>
</Compare>

<Callout title="Priority when multiple players call">
If two or more players want the same discard: **winning hand beats everything** → then **kong or pung** → then **chow**. Pung and kong ignore turn order; chow is only available to the player immediately after the discarder.
</Callout>

## The wall — and what happens when it runs out

The wall starts with **136 tiles** (144 minus the 8 bonus flower/season tiles). Each player draws 13 at the start, East draws 14 — so 70 tiles are dealt out, leaving **66 tiles** in the wall to draw from during play.

A few tiles are held back: the last 14 tiles in the wall are the *dead wall*, reserved as replacement draws for kongs. Players draw only from the *live wall* in front of them.

### When the wall empties

If the live wall runs out before anyone wins, the round ends in a **draw** — called a *goulash* or *chow kong* depending on the table. No points change hands.

<Callout title="What draw means for East">
A draw is neither a win nor a loss. East stays East and the round is replayed — no rotation. Only a win by another player (or East losing by deal) rotates the wind seat.
</Callout>

A wall that consistently runs dry usually means the table is playing too defensively — everyone holding safe tiles, nobody pressing toward a win. As the wall shrinks below 20 tiles, the calculus shifts: a risky discard gets safer (fewer rounds left for it to hit anyone), but chasing a rare hand becomes irrational.
