After the three suits, the set has 28 *honor* tiles: four winds and three dragons, with four copies of each.

## The four winds

Each wind tile carries one Chinese character — east, south, west, north — in the traditional clockwise order used at a mahjong table.

Note that the order around a Chinese table goes counter-clockwise — east to south is to the *right* of the dealer, not the left. We'll come back to this when we set up the table in chapter five.

## The three dragons

There are three dragons: *red*, *green*, and *white*.

<TileRow tiles={[
  { suit: 'dragon', value: 'red' },
  { suit: 'dragon', value: 'green' },
  { suit: 'dragon', value: 'white' },
]} />

- **Red dragon** (中) — the character means "middle" or "center".
- **Green dragon** (發) — meaning "to prosper" or "to develop". The same character on the right side of a calligraphic banner.
- **White dragon** (白板) — literally "white board". A blank tile with a thin frame — that's it.

<Callout title="Why honors matter">
In sequences (chow), only suit tiles can be used — never honors. But for triplets (pung), honors are not just allowed, they're *better*. A pung of dragons or your seat wind is worth scoring points all by itself. We'll see this in the scoring chapter.
</Callout>

## Counting check

Three suits × nine ranks × four copies = 108. Plus 4 winds × 4 + 3 dragons × 4 = 28 honors. That's 136. The remaining 8 tiles — flowers and seasons — are the subject of the next chapter.
