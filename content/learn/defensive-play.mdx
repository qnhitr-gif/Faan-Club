<ChapterHeader n={12} plain="When to" italic="fold." />

Most mahjong strategy is about winning. Defensive play is about *not losing* — specifically, not being the player whose discard completes an opponent's hand. Over a long session, the players who lose least tend to finish ahead of the players who win most.

<Section n={1} title="When to fold">

Folding means abandoning your hand plan and switching to purely safe discards until the round ends. It costs you the round's win potential but protects against large payments.

<Stages>
  <Stage n={1} name="Fold immediately" wall="" turns="" tone="red">
    Any opponent has three exposed sets. They need only one more tile. Any discard from you is high-risk.
  </Stage>
  <Stage n={2} name="Consider folding" wall="&lt;20" turns="" tone="amber">
    Wall under 20 tiles and your hand is still 2+ shanten. You likely won't reach tenpai in time — defend now.
  </Stage>
  <Stage n={3} name="Evaluate" wall="" turns="" tone="green">
    Holding demonstrably dangerous tiles with a marginal hand. Ask: is the potential win worth the payment risk?
  </Stage>
</Stages>

<Callout title="Folding is not giving up">
A fold that avoids paying 6 faan is worth more than winning at 3 faan. Players who can't fold when they should consistently overpay across a session.
</Callout>

</Section>

<Section n={2} title="Finding safe tiles">

<Pillars>
  <Pillar n={1} title="Already-discarded tiles">
    If an opponent has already thrown a tile, that exact tile is almost never their winning tile. Copy their discards back under pressure — this is the safest category.
  </Pillar>
  <Pillar n={2} title="Dead tiles">
    A tile with 3 copies visible has only 1 remaining. Four copies visible means completely safe — it cannot be anyone's wait.
  </Pillar>
  <Pillar n={3} title="1-4-7 neighbors">
    If an opponent threw a 4, tiles 1 and 7 in the same suit are probably not their winning tiles either. (See chapter 10 for full reasoning.)
  </Pillar>
  <Pillar n={4} title="Pair duplicates">
    If you're folding, discarding one tile from your own pair is relatively safe — you know exactly how many copies are visible.
  </Pillar>
</Pillars>

</Section>

<Section n={3} title="Suji — inferring safe tiles from discards">

*Suji* (筋) extends the 1-4-7 logic into a structured inference. When an opponent discards a tile, you can infer which tiles they are *probably* not waiting on.

If they discarded a **4**, they weren't holding 2–3 (they'd have wanted the 4 to complete 2–3–4) or 5–6 (they'd have wanted the 4 for 4–5–6). So tiles **1** and **7** in the same suit are likely safe — suji of the 4.

<Compare>
  <CompareSide title="The six suji pairs" tone="neutral">
    1 ↔ 4, 4 ↔ 7 (the 1-4-7 family)

    2 ↔ 5, 5 ↔ 8 (the 2-5-8 family)

    3 ↔ 6, 6 ↔ 9 (the 3-6-9 family)
  </CompareSide>
  <CompareSide title="When suji fails" tone="cautious">
    Suji doesn't cover pair waits, edge waits, or special hand shapes. An opponent could be waiting on an isolated pair that breaks the chow-logic assumption entirely. Treat suji as a probability filter, not a guarantee.
  </CompareSide>
</Compare>

</Section>

<Section n={4} title="Managing a losing hand">

When your hand clearly won't win — too far from tenpai, dangerous tiles to hold, strong opponents building fast — manage the loss rather than the win.

<Checklist items={[
  "Stop advancing your hand. Every new tile goes into safe-discard evaluation, not building.",
  "Prioritize tiles that have been discarded by the most dangerous player.",
  "Hold onto your safe tiles longer than feels right — being stuck beats paying.",
  "If two players are both dangerous, find the tile that's safe against both and hold it as long as possible.",
]} />

<Callout title="Defense improves gradually">
Most players develop offensive skills first and defensive skills years later. If you consciously practice the fold decision — even just learning to recognize three exposures — you're ahead of most beginners at the table.
</Callout>

</Section>
