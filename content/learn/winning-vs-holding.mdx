<ChapterHeader n={13} plain="Take it or" italic="hold." />

You're at tenpai. Someone discards your winning tile. The hand is worth 3 faan — enough to win. But one more tile could take you to 5 faan. Do you take the win or let it go?

This is the most common high-leverage decision in mahjong. Most players default to one answer. The right answer depends on five specific factors.

<Section n={1} title="The decision framework">

<Compare>
  <CompareSide title="Take the win — if any of these is true" tone="positive">
    - Wall has **≤ 20 tiles** remaining.
    - The upgrade tile is dead — 3 or 4 copies already visible.
    - An opponent has 2 or more exposures.
    - The upgrade needs **2 or more specific tiles** in sequence.
    - Anyone at the table shows a third exposure.
    - You're behind and need the guaranteed points now.
  </CompareSide>
  <CompareSide title="Hold for bigger — only when all of these are true" tone="cautious">
    - Wall has **> 20 tiles** remaining.
    - Upgrade needs only **1 specific tile**.
    - That tile has **2 or more live copies** in the wall.
    - No opponent has more than 1 exposure.
    - Holding doesn't force you to discard a dangerous tile.
  </CompareSide>
</Compare>

The conditions for holding require every factor to align. In practice, by tenpai, at least one condition has usually failed — wall is short, or the upgrade tile has been seen, or another player is advanced.

</Section>

<Section n={2} title="The upgrade tile problem">

When calculating whether to hold, be precise about what you're waiting for.

<Pillars>
  <Pillar n={1} title="One tile needed">
    How many copies are live? Subtract visible tiles. If 5-Bamboo is your upgrade and you've seen two discarded, only 2 remain among ~20 wall tiles — roughly 10% per draw. Is that worth passing a guaranteed win?
  </Pillar>
  <Pillar n={2} title="Two tiles needed">
    You need a sequence — draw A, then B. The probability compounds. Almost never worth holding past the midgame.
  </Pillar>
  <Pillar n={3} title="The forced discard">
    Holding for an upgrade means drawing a tile and discarding one. If that forced discard is dangerous to an opponent, you're now risking a loss — not just a missed upgrade. Only hold if you can identify a safe discard for whatever you'd draw next.
  </Pillar>
</Pillars>

</Section>

<Section n={3} title="Worked example">

Wall: 28 tiles. Tenpai waiting on 6-Dot, common hand + self-draw = 3 faan. Drawing 8-Dot instead would complete pure one-suit = 7 faan.

Check the framework:
- Wall > 20 ✓
- Upgrade needs 1 tile ✓ (8-Dot)
- 8-Dot: one copy visible in discards, three remain ✓
- No opponent has more than 1 exposure ✓
- You hold characters as safe discards for the next draw ✓

All five conditions pass. Hold for the upgrade.

Same situation but wall = 15 tiles. Condition 1 fails. **Take the win.**

<Callout title="Don't romanticize the big hand">
Limit hands and 13-faan wins make great stories. They also require passing on many smaller wins, creating opportunities to deal into opponents. Players with the best long-term results play consistently, not heroically.
</Callout>

</Section>
